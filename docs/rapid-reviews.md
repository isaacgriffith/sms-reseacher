# Rapid Reviews

Rapid Reviews are secondary studies aiming to provide research evidence to support decision-making in practice. Rapid Reviews must be conducted taking into account the contraints inherent to pracitcal environments, such as time and effort. Rapid Reviews usually deliver evidence in a more timely manner, with lower costs, and reporting results through more appealing mediums.

**Main Goal of a Rapid Review**: to privde evidence to support decision-making towards the solution, or at least attenuation, of issues practitioners face in practice.

- Rapid Reviews should be performed in close collaboration with practitioners, bounded to practical problems, and conducted within practitioners context.
- Rapid Reviews are intended to reduce costs and time of heavywight methods
- Rapid Reviews should be reported through mediums appealing to practitiones.

**Note**:
1. Rapid Reviews are not ad-hoc literature reviews
2. Rapid Reviews are not an excuse for absence of scientific rigor

Rather, Rapid Reviews must be systematic, by means of following a well-defined protocol

## Key Differences between Rapid Reviews and Systematic Reviews

- **Problem**: Rapid reviews are bounded to a practical problem and conducted within a practical context
- **Research Questions**: In Rapid Reviews, these lead to answers that help solving or at least attenuating the practitioners' problem. Exploratory questions aiming to identify which are the strategies and their effectiveness to deal with practitioners problem are one of the the gold standards
- **Protocol**: Must have a document formalizing the protocol, same as in Systematic Reviews
- **Time Frame**: In Rapid Reviews, days or weeks vs. Months or years
- **Search Strategy**: In Rapid Reviews:
  - May use few or just one search source (e.g., Scopus)
  - May limit search by publication year, language, and study design
- **Selection Procedure**: In Rapid Reviews:
  - Can be conducted by a single person
  - The inclusion/exclusion criteria can be more restrictive aiming to focus on primary studies conducted in contexts similar to the one motivating the Rapid Review. (e.g., studies with small/medium/large companies, with companies in countries unders specific laws, with open source projects only, etc.)
- **Quality Appraisal**: In Rapid Reviews, this is conducted by a single person, or not conducted at all.
- **Extraction Procedure**: In Rapid Reviews, this is usually conducted by a single person to reduce time and effort
- **Synthesis Procedure**: In Rapid Reviews, narrative summaries are the most common way to synthesize evidence
- **Report**: In Rapid Reviews, alternative mediums that better fit practitioners' needs are used (e.g., Evidence Briefings)

## Process

- Phase 1. Planning

- Phase 2. Conducting Review

- Phase 3. Reporting the Review

## Planning a Rapid Review

- Comprehends the creation of a protocol to define all the decisions and procedures demanded to conduct the Rapid REview.
- The protocol must
  - Identify the practical problem the review intends to provide evidence for
  - Identify the roles of each stakeholder aiming to guarantee practitioners' active participation
  - Identify the demand and need for the review

### Demand for a Rapid Review

The demand for a Rapid Review can emerge from different sources under different contexts. Some possible arrangements are:

- Practitioners ask for a Rapid Review
- Researcher aligns his/her research agenda based on a practical problem
- Researcher prospects a research agenda based on a practical problem

### Defining the Problem

Close collaboration with practitioners is crucial to define the problem that will drive the Rapid Review

- Sometimes, the problem is not already well-defined
- Researchers can use qualitative research methods such as interviews or focus groups to better understand the context and the (eventually hidden) problems

Sometimes, you identify a practical problem but there are no studies approaching such problem, so an Rapid Review will noto be viable, and you may need to find another problem.

### Defining the Research Questions

- Research questions in Rapid Reviews are as important as they are in Systematic Reviews
- Once defined, all effort is towards answering them. However, to provide useful answers, one must ask meaningful questions.
  - In Rapid Reviews, answers are useful when they help practitioners to solve or at least attenuate their practical problem.
- Thus, research questions should be defined in close collaboration with practitioners.
  - Questions aiming to identify research gaps or to provide more general insights into the research community should be avoided and left to Systematic Reviews.
  - Rapid Reviews should provide answers bounded to the practical context they are inserted into.
- While each problem demands different kinds of questions and approaches to investigate them. Experience has shown that exploratory questions aiming to identify strategies to deal with a particular problem are the cornerstone of Rapid Reviews since the most important thing to practitioners under time constraints is to discover strategies, supported by evidence, to solve their problems.
- Examples of such questions are:
  - What are the strategies to improve customer collaboration ins software development practice? What are their effectiveness?
  - What are the strategies to improve software development teams motivation? What are their effectiveness?

- Other research questions are possible, if answering them helps practitioners toward the solution of their problem
- Examples include:
  - What are the benefits of customer collaboration in software development practice?
  - What are the problems caused by low customer collaboration in software development practice?

### Defining Stakeholders Roles

A Rapid Review is a joint initiative between researchers and practitioners. Thus, active participation of both sides is both important and mandatory.

- The **researchers' role** is to guarantee the methodological consistency and transparency
- The **practitioners' role** is to make sure that the research is bounded to an actual practical problem, so the evidence will be useful

### Creating the Protocol

The protocol has the goal of specifying all the methodological steps that understake the review.

- The protocol is one of the most important elements making the Rapid Review systematic.
- The protocol must be well-documented

The key components of the protocol are the same as in Systematic Literature Reviews

## Conducting the Review

Transparency is the Golden Standard in Rapid Reviews.

- Regardless of strategies employed to reduce cost and/or time to conduct the review, limitations and threats to validity must be reported in the protocol.

### Search Strategy

Rapid Reviews tend to only utilize a single search strategy (from those avialble as part of Systematic Literature Reviews or Systematic Mapping Studies).

- They tend to use only one approach from a single source. For example Database Search with a single index such as Scopus or Google Scholar

There are additional approaches that, if employed, can reduce the effort placed on conducting Rapid Reviews:

1. Limiting the search by date
2. Restricting the language in which the paper is written
3. Focusing on a given geographical area
4. limiting the primary studies according to their research method (e.g., controlled experiments only, or case studies only)

It is important to note that those approaches may lead to relevant studies being excluded and, as a consequence, reducing the coverage of the review.

If such strategies are adopted, threats to validity must be transparently reported.

### Selection Procedure

Since Rapid Reviews are bound to a practical context, one may define restrictive inclusion/exclusion criteria.

The goal here is two fold:
- To reduce the amount of studies to screen
- To provide evidence that better fits practitioners' needs

**Examples**
- The study must not be related to large companies
- The study must not be related to distributed teams
- The study must not be related to croud source software development
- The study must not be related to open source software development

Defining restrictive inclusion/exclusion criteria may reduce the time and effort need to conduct the review. However, this procedure does not necessarily incur threats to validity. Rather, it may be considered good practice to consider evidence only from primary studies conducted in similar contexts to that of the performed review.

Highly conextualized studies are considered one of the best ways to have impact in practice.

**Note**: Since Rapid Reviews are often performed by a single reviewer, this can introduce selection bias. To avoid this, a second reviewer can be used. Either way, this should be discussed in limitations and threats to validity.

#### Typical Review Process

Using the inclusion/exclusion criteria the following steps are used to assess the inclusion of primary studies

1. Review titles - exclude any paper with a title that clearly indicates the research fits within the purview of the review
2. Review abstracts - include any paper with an abstract that clearly indicates the research fits within the purview of the review
3. Review full paper - include any paper that describes research that clearly fits within the purview of the review

### Quality Appraisal

While this is used as part of Systematic Reviews, this can be either totally skipped as part of Rapid Reviews.

Antoher less radical approach is to focus only on those studies that are published in peer-reviewed conferences or journals. However, while this will increase the quality of the evidence it does could exclude relevant studies published in less reputable venues or on arXiv.


### Extraction Procedure

Data extraction can be conducted by a single reviewer in a Rapid Review, as long as the inherent biases are transparently reported.

Additionally, note that if a study has missing data, it should likely be excluded.

### Synthesis Procedure

Knowledge synthesis is one of the most important stesps.

Typically in Rapid Reviews, lightweight approaches for knowledge synthesis are used. Predominantly Narrative Synthesis.

## Reporting the Review

Reporting and sisseminating knowledge produced with Rapid Reviews are as important as conducting the Rapid Review itself.

Since Rapid Reviews target practitioners, one should consider that not all information that is crucial to researchers is also relevant to practitioners (e.g., research method, background, related work, etc.).

Thus, Rapid Reviews, must be reported in a more straightforward way, focusing on results and recommendations, so pracititioners can easily consume the information to support their decision-making.

### Evidence Briefings

Evidence Briefings are one-page documents reporting the main findings of the Rapid Review.

They should follow the template at: http://cin.ufpe.br/eseg/briefings

**Components**:

1. The **title** of an Evidence Briefing should be as concise as possible, and comprise one or two lines only. Titles with more than two lines should be avoided since they might reduce document space to report Rapid Review findings.
2. To fill the Evidence Briefing's **summary**, we suggest researchers to adopt the following structure:
   
   ```
   This briefing reports scientific evidence on <RESEARCH GOAL>.
   ```

   The summary should span only a few lines.

   Example:
   ```
   This briefing reports scientific evidence on the challenges involved in using Scrum for global software
   development (GSD) projects, and strategies available to deal with them.
   ```
3. The **findings** section is the most important one. It should list the main findings of the Rapid Review. When writing the findings, we recommend to use one finding per paragraph. Bullets to highlight important points as well as charts, figures, and tables are welcome since they make the findings even easier to read. Findings should be short sentences, straight to the point. The findings sections should not include information about the research method. The idea of the Evidence Briefing is to quickly comunicate the main findings of a Rapid Review to practitiones. If they have interest they can refer to the complementary material reference shown in item 5.
4. The **box at the right side** of the Evidence Briefing should be filled with information about the Evidence Briefing's target audience, clarifications about what information is included, and what is not included in the Evidence Briefing.
5. The **reference** to complementary material should be palced at the bottom of the Evidence Briefing. It may be a link to a webpage containing at least the following documents/information: the Rapid Review protocol document and a list of references to the primary studies included in the Rapid Review
6. **Logos** of universities, software companies, and any other institutions involved in the Rapid Review initiative should be placed at the very top of the Evidence Briefing document. This publicizes the insitutions producing Evidence Briefings and might make practitioners search for more Rapid Reviews on institutions' websites.
